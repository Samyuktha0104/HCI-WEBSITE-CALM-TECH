Combating Doomscrolling in the Attention Economy

By Samyuktha Mandampully, Lucero Fuentes Ramos and Patricia Sharleen.

Low Fidelity Prototype

We began the design process by creating 12 initial low-fidelity prototypes that explored various aspects of the app’s core features. These early prototypes allowed us to visualize how users might interact and benefit from different functionalities helping them avoid doomscrolling.

After evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of these designs, we identified the most promising concepts and refined them further using the 10+10 ideation method. This helped us generate a diverse set of design alternatives while maintaining a focus on clarity and user experience.

To make the concepts more engaging and relatable, we represented them through story-based sketches that depict potential user interactions. The initial collection consists of 12 prototype images expressing 10 distinct design ideas, each exploring a unique approach to mindful digital engagement.

Sketch version 1

In the first sketch, four models are presented to promote more mindful and calm phone use while doomscrolling. The first model uses soft colors around the screen edges to subtly alert the user about their usage time. The second displays a small image or icon indicating the elapsed time. The third gradually dims the screen brightness to encourage a break, and the fourth features a character that pops up to notify the user when they have been using their device for too long.

Sketch version 2

In the paper prototype, we explored different ways of representing time spent on apps. The first and second diagrams both use bar-like visual indicators to show the duration of usage for a particular app. The third diagram introduces an alternative approach, where the scrolling speed is deliberately slowed down, forcing users to become more aware of their engagement with the content. The fourth diagram utilizes decreasing brightness to create added friction.

Sketch version 3

Paper prototype of Instagram UIs, with alongside solution UIs overlaid and time progression emphasized. The time progression is depicted in 15 minute intervals from 10:00 am to 10:45 am across four models. The first one shows an unchanged/regular Instagram UI at the beginning of the interaction. The second shows a timer icon pop up in the right upper corner (periphery visual cue) after 15 minutes. After 30 minutes, a lightning bolt takes up a larger space and pops up with a question “Continue?” with response choices ‘Yes’, ‘No’ and ‘Redirect’. Based on the choices, the system either continues background activities, terminates, or redirects to a different task. The fourth model depicts another peripheral notification saying “Black & white mode activated” where the screen gradually becomes monochrome.

Functionality-Based Prototypes

After developing our initial low-fidelity sketches, we moved on to create prototypes focused on three key features that we wanted to integrate into our app:

  1. Providing users with a sense of time and the amount of content consumed.
  2. Designing interventions to help users become aware of and regulate their scrolling behavior.
  3. Offering customizability so users can personalize their experience and control how these features appear.

Through this stage, we explored how different sections of the app would look and function with these new features enabled. We also designed visual mockups of how the social media platform (in this case, Instagram) would appear once these mindful interaction elements were applied. Each prototype highlights how these features can encourage more balanced engagement while maintaining the familiar experience of social media use.

Sketch Version 1

The first five sketches illustrate the time-awareness functionality, exploring different visual approaches to help users understand how long they have been scrolling or how much content they’ve consumed. Each variation tests a distinct method for representing time — through subtle on-screen cues, edge color changes, visual timers, or animated indicators — allowing us to identify which format feels most natural and least intrusive to users.

Sketch Version 2

The second sketch illustrates the intervention functionality. The image shows four variations of interventions designed to subtly interrupt prolonged scrolling and encourage mindful breaks. Two main strategies are explored: one where the Instagram feed gradually turns monochrome after extended use, and another where the brightness of the feed is reduced. Each variation tests how visual cues and changes in display affect user awareness and promote calmer phone usage.

Sketch Version 3

The third sketch illustrates the scroll-slowdown functionality. After the user reaches a pre-set time limit for Instagram usage, the feed gradually slows down, making it harder to scroll quickly. This intervention encourages users to become more aware of their usage, reduces impulsive scrolling, and helps them take a mindful break without abruptly stopping their session.

Sketch Version 4

This set of sketches illustrates different sections of the app focusing on the third functionality customizability, showing how the proposed features integrate with the user interface. The Home Page allows users to control the amount of time spent on each app. The Friends Page displays usage and compares it with friends. The Controls Page lets users enable or disable features such as monochrome mode, slow scroll, time display, and brightness control via toggle buttons. Finally, the Profile Page shows personal details and earned badges, offering a personalized experience.

Usability Goals

The table below summarizes the usability objectives, their measurement, and the rationale for our app design. These serve as benchmarks for evaluating key tasks during user testing.

Goal Measurement Rationale
Ease of Use
Users must be able to complete all key tasks within the stipulated time of the usability testing session, with minimal external guidance.		 Percentage of tasks completed and average time taken. Fewer errors and shorter completion time indicate higher ease of use. Ensures effortless interaction with features to promote self-awareness and behavioral change. Avoids discouraging users with a steep learning curve.
Efficiency of Use
Users must carry out all specified actions quickly and error-free.		 Time taken to complete tasks, and tracking repeated or redundant interactions (e.g., wrong taps). Minimizes time spent managing the app, allowing users to focus on actual feedback. Avoids the app becoming a distraction.
Learnability
New users must understand and use all primary features without extensive instructions.		 Observation of task exploration, number of prompts required, and improvement in task completion speed. High learnability ensures continued engagement and retention, enabling users to set goals and interpret progress intuitively.
Customizability
Users can tailor experience: screen-time goals, notification tones, themes, etc.		 Steps and time taken to modify settings; post-test survey on 5-point Likert scale for satisfaction. Personalization enhances ownership and autonomy, supporting long-term adherence to self-regulation goals.
Engagement
Maintain motivation through progress visualization, prompts, and gamified challenges.		 User response to engagement features, post-test feedback, retention rates, and time spent in app. Fosters intrinsic motivation, helping users view progress positively and stay consistent in reducing screen time.
Error Accommodation
System provides clear feedback and allows undoing/adjusting actions.		 Frequency/type of errors, time taken to recover, clarity of confirmation dialogs and messages. Encourages exploration without fear of irreversible mistakes, reducing user frustration.
Utility
All features must be relevant and valuable to reducing Instagram screen time.		 User ratings on usefulness; ≥80% should indicate features contribute to intended outcomes. Ensures all design elements are meaningful; avoids unnecessary features that dilute effectiveness.
User Satisfaction
Visual design, responsiveness, and feedback must meet or exceed expectations.		 Post-test surveys and follow-up interviews with slider-based ratings. Satisfied users integrate the app into daily life and recommend it to others; correlates with perceived usability and trust.
User Safety and Privacy
App must securely handle user data and ensure psychological safety.		 Check user concerns about data handling/privacy; ratings on perceived safety and trust. Promotes trust and honest reflection on habits; essential for meaningful behavioral change.

Benchmarks

The following benchmark tasks were designed to evaluate the usability of the proposed system in relation to the defined goals. Each task corresponds to essential user interactions that represent the primary functionality of the app: tracking screen time, setting goals, managing reminders, customizing preferences, and reflecting on progress. These benchmarks will help determine how effectively the system supports users in becoming more aware of and controlling their scroll time usage, and can be conscious of their digital engagement.

Task Goals Description Success Criteria Metrics
Set and Adjust Screen-Time Limits Ease of Use, Efficiency, Learnability, Error Accommodation User sets a personalized limit for scrolling usage. Task completed with minimal confusion. Completion time, number of errors, recovery time, and clarity of feedback.
Enable and Customize Interventions Learnability, Customizability, Engagement, Satisfaction, Error Accommodation User enables and configures the four available interventions: Monochrome mode, Slow Scroll, Brightness Reduction, and Monitor Time. User successfully activates, customizes, and experiences each intervention as intended, demonstrating understanding of their function. Completion rate, task time, number of prompts, user reaction, and satisfaction level.
View and Reflect on Usage Data Engagement, Behavioral Regulation, Satisfaction User locates and interprets daily or weekly screen-time data within the app dashboard, then reflects briefly on their usage. User finds and understands the data correctly and provides a short reflection or insight. Completion time, navigation errors, reflection quality, and qualitative feedback.
Respond to Gradual Behavior Cues Engagement, Utility, Satisfaction User experiences progressive interventions over time and reacts appropriately. User adjusts scrolling behavior or pauses without irritation or confusion. Response time, continuation rate, and emotional tone.
End-of-Session Reflection Engagement, Utility, Satisfaction User reviews their session summary and decides on next steps. User completes reflection and expresses insight or motivation to continue. Completion rate, reflection depth, satisfaction score.

Observer Briefing for both prototypes

As an observer, your primary responsibility is to quietly watch how participants interact with the app designed to help reduce Instagram screen time. Avoid offering assistance, explanations, or suggestions unless the participant is completely unable to continue.

During the session, pay close attention to:

  • How the participant navigates through the interface to explore features.
  • The clarity and intuitiveness of the interface as users attempt to complete key actions like setting limits, reviewing progress, or customizing their experience.
  • Visible signs of confusion, hesitation, or frustration, including pauses, repeated actions, or verbal expressions of difficulty, and how the participant chooses to respond to these situations.

Your goal is to gather detailed observations that shed light on the system’s ease of use, efficiency, engagement, and ability to handle errors. Specifically, record:

  • Any usability challenges the participant encounters.
  • The approximate time taken to complete major tasks.
  • Behavioral cues and feedback that reflect the participant’s comfort, satisfaction, or frustration.

Please remain neutral and unobtrusive throughout the process. The goal is to observe authentic user behavior and capture insights that will help evaluate the design’s effectiveness and identify areas for improvement.

User Training Documentation

Since the goal of this usability test is to evaluate how intuitive, learnable, and user-friendly the prototype design is, participants were not provided with any prior instructions, manuals, or demonstrations on how to use the app or its features. Given that participants already possess familiarity with Instagram and its interface, they were expected to rely on this prior experience to navigate and complete tasks within the prototype. Before starting the test, participants were only briefed on the specific benchmark tasks they were required to perform, such as viewing their screen-time statistics, setting time limits, enabling reminders, and customizing their experience, but no further operational details were shared. This approach ensures that user performance reflects the prototype’s natural ease of use and intuitiveness, allowing us to accurately assess how effectively the design supports users in managing and reducing their Instagram screen time without explicit instruction.

Test Tasks

The usability study focuses on evaluating how effectively the app supports users in tracking and managing their digital habits across multiple platforms. Participants will complete a series of tasks designed to explore the system’s main features, from basic navigation to setting limits and responding to interventions. Each task is designed to test different features of the system, capturing both the ease of use and the quality of insights gained.

  1. User Log In
    Participants log into the app using a username and password. This task ensures that users can access the system successfully and locate the main interface.
    Steps:
    • Open the app.
    • Enter username and password.
    • Confirm successful login.
  2. Explore the App
    Participants familiarize themselves with the app layout, including the main windows and features. This task helps assess how intuitively users can navigate between sections such as Time Limits, Controls, Usage, Friends, Profile, and Settings.
    Steps:
    • Navigate through the main window for Time Limits settings.
    • Open the Controls window (Prototype 1 only).
    • Check the Usage window to view app-specific scrolling times.
    • Explore Friends features to see shared goals.
    • Visit the Profile window to view badges, streaks, and friend stats.
    • Open Settings to explore language options, user info, and logout functionality.
  3. Set Time Limits
    Participants set limits for daily usage to see how easily the app allows control over digital habits.
    Steps:
    • Navigate to the “Time Limits” section.
    • Set daily or app-specific scrolling limits.
    • Confirm that limits are saved correctly.
  4. Enable an Intervention in Controls (Prototype 1 only)
    Participants test interventions designed to influence usage behavior. For this experiment, they will activate one intervention at a time.
    Steps:
    • Open the “Controls” window.
    • Select a single intervention: Monochrome, Slow Scroll, Brightness, or Monitor Time.
    • Verify that the selected intervention is active while others remain off.
    • Observe the effect of the intervention on app usage.
  5. React to Contextual Events (Prototype 2 only)
    Participants experience dynamic changes in the app interface that simulate real-world interruptions.
    Steps:
    • A clock reminds the user of elapsed time.
    • Screen monochromy gradually decreases.
    • Scrolling becomes slower or slightly laggy.
  6. Check Time Usage
    Participants review daily app usage statistics to evaluate how clearly information is presented.
    Steps:
    • Access the “Dashboard” tab.
    • Review total time spent and app-specific breakdown.
    • Review past usage statistics and visualizations.

Test Script

Introduction

This section is for the document; it explains what the facilitator will say to participants:

  • “Hello ____________, and thank you for joining this session. My name is ______________, and I am one of the investigators for this study, which explores the use of mobile apps to reduce excessive scrolling and manage social media habits.”
  • “Before we begin, please remember that your participation is voluntary, and you can stop the session at any time without any consequence.”
  • “All your responses and recordings will remain confidential and will be used only for academic purposes.”
  • “You have already reviewed and signed the consent form. Do you have any questions before we start?”
  • “Here’s what we’ll do today: first, I will ask you a few questions about your habits and experiences with social media and doomscrolling. Then, you’ll interact with two prototypes of our app through a series of tasks. Finally, we will reflect together on your impressions and experiences.”
  • “If everything is clear, we can begin.”

Interview Questions

User Habits
  • Do you know what doomscrolling is?
  • Do you think you doomscroll, and if so, has it had a negative impact on your life?
  • What makes you continue scrolling even when you’re no longer enjoying the content?
Impact and Motivation
  • What usually helps you pause or take a break during long social media sessions?
  • What kinds of features or interventions do you notice in apps that actually make you change your behavior?
  • In your opinion, what makes it easier or harder to stick to limits on app usage?

Task Script

Prototype 1

“Now we will move to the activity portion. You will interact with Prototype 1 of our app, which includes controls for interventions. You will complete a series of tasks while I observe how intuitive the app is and how you respond to it. Please use the app as naturally as you would at home. I will record your interactions to analyze usability and task completion.”

  • User Log In: “Please open the app and log in using your username and password. This ensures you can access the system and locate the main interface.”
  • Explore the App: “Take some time to explore the main windows and features. Navigate through:
    • Time Limits settings
    • Controls window (where you can mix interventions, but for this experiment, test one at a time)
    • Usage and Friends
    • Profile (badges, streaks, friend stats)
    • Settings (language options, user info, logout)
  • Set Time Limits: “Now, go to the Time Limits section and set limits for daily or app-specific usage. Make sure the limits are saved correctly.”
  • Enable an Intervention in Controls: “Next, open the Controls window. For this session, activate only one intervention at a time: Monochrome, Slow Scroll, Brightness, or Monitor Time. Verify that the selected intervention is active while others remain off and observe the effect on your app usage.”
  • Check Time Usage: “Finally, access the Dashboard and review your daily usage statistics. Look at the total time spent, breakdown by app, and past usage patterns to see how clearly the information is presented.”
Prototype 2

“Now we will move to Prototype 2. This version does not have the Controls window; instead, it introduces dynamic contextual events during usage. The purpose is to observe how you respond to real-time changes.”

  • Explore the App: “Take some time to explore the main windows and features. Navigate through:
    • Time Limits settings
    • Usage and Friends
    • Profile (badges, streaks, friend stats)
    • Settings (language options, user info, logout)
  • Set Time Limits: “Go to the Time Limits section and set your daily or app-specific scrolling limits.”
  • React to Contextual Events: “While using the app, you will experience the following:
    • A clock reminds you of elapsed time.
    • Screen monochromy gradually decreases.
    • Scrolling becomes slower or slightly laggy.
    Please continue using the app naturally, and I will observe how you respond.”
  • Check Time Usage: “Review your daily usage statistics on the Dashboard. Notice the clarity of information, app-specific breakdowns, and past usage patterns.”

Reflection and Feedback Questions

“Now let’s reflect on your experience interacting with the app prototype.”

  • How clear and understandable were the interventions?
  • Which prototype did you prefer, and why?
  • Did the app help you become more aware of your scrolling habits?
  • Were there moments where the app felt intrusive or annoying?
  • How intuitive did you find the interface layout and navigation?
  • Did interacting with the dashboard and usage summaries help you understand your habits better?
  • Was there anything confusing or distracting about the app?
  • How comfortable did you feel using the app for a longer session?
  • If you could suggest one improvement, what would it be?
  • Do you have any other thoughts or comments about your experience today?
  • Which intervention or combination of interventions do you feel would be most effective for controlling your doomscrolling habits?

Closing Statement

“Thank you very much for your time and insights today. Your feedback is extremely valuable and will help improve tools designed to reduce doomscrolling and promote healthy social media habits. If you would like to receive updates about this study, please let me know. That concludes our session!”
